# Victoria Free Public Transport Initiative 2026

## Survey and Traffic Analysis Technical Summary

This repository provides a public research summary of an ongoing evaluation of Victoria's 2026 free and discounted public transport policy.

The Victorian Government made public transport free between 31 March and 31 May 2026 in response to rising fuel prices and cost-of-living pressures. From June 2026, public transport fares were reduced by 50% for the remainder of the year.

This project examines how these fare changes influenced travel behaviour, public transport use and road traffic patterns.

## Current status

Version 1.0, June 2026

This version summarises early findings from:

- A survey of 1,028 Victorian residents conducted during the free public transport period.
- Traffic monitoring data from five major Melbourne corridors:
  - Eastern Freeway
  - Monash Freeway
  - Nepean Highway corridor
  - Tullamarine Freeway
  - West Gate corridor

Further updates will be added as the 50% discounted fare period continues during 2026.

## Key findings

The survey found that during the free public transport period:

| Indicator | Result |
|---|---:|
| Increased public transport use | 39.5% |
| Reduced car use | 28.8% |
| Replaced some car trips with public transport | 38.2% |
| Increased overall travel | 16.1% |
| Supported affordable public transport during economic pressure | 77.4% |

Traffic analysis showed that four of the five monitored corridors recorded lower traffic volumes than their matched March baseline.

| Corridor | Average traffic change |
|---|---:|
| Tullamarine | -6.4% |
| Monash | -3.2% |
| Eastern | -2.2% |
| Nepean | -1.3% |
| West Gate | +3.1% |

## Interpretation

The findings suggest free public transport encouraged many Victorians to try public transport more often and replace some car trips. However, the effects on road traffic were modest and varied across Melbourne's transport network.

The policy appears to have been welcomed as a cost-of-living measure, but fare reductions alone are unlikely to produce large-scale shifts away from private cars without parallel improvements in service quality, reliability, frequency and capacity.

## Data and privacy

This repository does not include raw survey data.

Only aggregated findings, summary tables, figures and methodological notes are provided.

SCATS and TIRTL traffic data are publicly available through Transport Victoria open data sources. Survey results are reported only in aggregate form.
